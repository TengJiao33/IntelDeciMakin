# APSS peer contribution organization notes

Date: 2026-06-29

Purpose: respond to the concern that the current APSS contribution framing is still too accessory. This note does not try to sell APSS. It first studies how nearby decision / MCDA peers organize their innovations, then diagnoses what APSS is currently missing.

## What Strong Peer Papers Actually Do

### Pattern 1: Turn a recurring analyst question into a concrete system

Peer example: Cinelli et al. (2022), MCDA-MSS.

They start from a direct decision-analysis question:

> Which MCDA method, or subset of methods, should be used for a given decision-making problem?

Then they do not merely propose a "framework". They materialize the contribution as a DSS with:

- a taxonomy of 156 characteristics;
- a large database of MCDA methods;
- a procedure for no-match cases;
- a procedure for reducing a too-large set of recommended methods;
- a diagnostic use case for detecting method-selection errors in literature cases.

Why it works:

- The contribution object is visible: MCDA-MSS.
- The internal artifacts are countable: taxonomy, database, matching logic, question strategy.
- The validation is supportive, not the only novelty.

Lesson for APSS:

If we claim "adaptive method/structure selection", we need an actual selection object. A vague APSS framework will look weaker than MCDA-MSS unless it specifies what it selects, from what evidence, through what mechanism, and what output it gives beyond a chosen method name.

### Pattern 2: Propose a method plus new internal measures

Peer example: Pelissari & Duarte (2020), SMAA-S-Choquet.

Their gap is precise:

- Choquet capacity identification was mostly studied for ranking.
- Very few similar approaches existed for multicriteria sorting.
- Existing SMAA sorting measures were too individual-alternative-oriented and lacked reverse-analysis measures.

Their contributions are exactly three:

- SMAA-S-Choquet for Choquet capacity identification in sorting;
- Scenario Acceptability Index, which evaluates whole assignment scenarios instead of isolated category assignments;
- Scenario Central Capacity vector, which supports reverse analysis of the capacity vector that makes a scenario possible.

Why it works:

- They do not say "we validate Choquet better".
- They invent method-internal objects that the reader can name and compute.
- Their empirical example demonstrates the objects.

Lesson for APSS:

Our third contribution cannot be "false-complexity-aware validation" if it only lives in the experiment section. It has to become a method-internal object, for example a structure acceptability index, a central structure explanation, or a structure necessity/possibility relation.

### Pattern 3: Relax one assumption inside a named model family

Peer example: learning MR-Sort from non-monotone data.

The paper does not claim to solve all adaptive decision learning. It picks a concrete assumption:

- existing Inv-MR-Sort learns parameters under monotone criteria;
- real criteria may be gain, cost, single-peaked, or single-valley;
- the criterion type itself is unknown.

The contribution is a MIP-based method that jointly learns:

- MR-Sort parameters;
- criterion type: gain, cost, single-peaked, or single-valley.

Why it works:

- The model family is fixed.
- The relaxed assumption is explicit.
- The new learned object is explicit.

Lesson for APSS:

If APSS remains cross-family, it must name the structural variables it learns at the same level of explicitness. "Adaptive" is not enough. We need to say, for example, the system learns whether each criterion is relevant, monotone, nonmonotone, interaction-bearing, or unnecessary under a decision task.

### Pattern 4: Embed a selection problem inside preference disaggregation

Peer example: Zhou et al. (2025), preference disaggregation with criteria selection.

Their gap is also concrete:

- DMs may not evaluate alternatives using all criteria because of cognitive limitations.
- Existing attribute reduction is often a preprocessing procedure independent of model learning.
- MCDA needs criteria selection together with the preference model.

Their method object:

- an embedded criteria selection model based on preference disaggregation and regularization;
- 0-1 variables indicate whether a criterion is selected;
- the value function complexity is decomposed into deviation from linearity plus number of selected marginal value functions;
- consistent and inconsistent preference-information cases are handled.

Why it works:

- The paper learns a new object inside a familiar MCDA model: the supporting criteria set.
- It is not just "regularization improves accuracy"; it is "regularization reveals which criteria the DM may rely on".

Lesson for APSS:

Criteria selection is not enough for us. It is already a peer contribution. But its organization is useful: APSS needs a concrete hidden object to infer. A likely hidden object is the preference-structure configuration, not only parameters.

### Pattern 5: Convert MCDA methods into learnable architectures

Peer example: Martyn & Kadziński (2023), deep preference learning in MCDA.

Their gap:

- MCDA models are interpretable but often designed for small examples.
- ML handles large inconsistent data but is less interpretable.

Their method object:

- ANN versions of several MCDA-compatible scoring approaches, including UTADIS-like, Choquet, OWA, TOPSIS, PROMETHEE, and ELECTRE-related structures;
- architecture components designed for interpretability;
- AdamW-based optimization over parameters;
- benchmark comparison against LR, KLR, MORE, LMT, UTADIS, MR-Sort MIP/META, etc.

Important future-work signal:

- they explicitly mention learning weights over different approaches, ensembles of near-best models, and online settings.

Lesson for APSS:

This is the closest warning. If we say "a network activates the best MCDA component", Martyn & Kadziński already point in that direction. To survive, APSS must be decision-journal specific and sharper, for example by making structural uncertainty and decision recommendation the object, not just model activation for predictive accuracy.

### Pattern 6: Make scalability the concrete bottleneck

Peer example: Sobrie, Mousseau & Pirlot (2019), MR-Sort parameter learning.

Their gap:

- MCDA learning sets were usually small.
- existing exact MIP learning can become computationally hard.

Their contribution:

- a population-based metaheuristic for learning MR-Sort parameters from large assignment-example sets;
- comparison with MIP, UTADIS, and Choquistic Regression.

Lesson for APSS:

Benchmarking is accepted when it demonstrates a concrete bottleneck, such as scalability or predictive competitiveness of a named learner. But benchmark alone is not the innovation.

## Cross-Peer Contribution Grammar

The recurring grammar is:

1. A practical decision-analysis question.
2. A specific limitation or assumption in existing methods.
3. A named method object.
4. One or two internal constructs that did not exist in that exact role.
5. Formalization or algorithmic formulation.
6. Experiments, case study, or benchmark as support.
7. Limitations and future extensions.

The important part is step 3 and step 4. Without them, the paper sounds like an umbrella.

## Diagnosis Of Our Current APSS Framing

The current APSS framing has two acceptable high-level claims:

1. It moves learning one layer up, from parameter learning inside a selected model to preference-structure selection.
2. It tries to govern interpretable decision structures rather than simply maximize predictive accuracy.

But the weak point is now clear:

> APSS does not yet have a sufficiently concrete method object or internal construct.

The previous contribution 3, "false-complexity-aware validation", is not wrong, but it reads like an experiment protocol. It does not yet read like a peer-level innovation because it is not a computable decision-support object in the method.

## What A More Peer-Like APSS Contribution Would Need

### Candidate concrete object A: Preference-Structure Acceptability Analysis

Possible name:

- Preference-Structure Acceptability Analysis, PSAA.

Core idea:

Extend the ROR/SMAA intuition from "all compatible parameter instances within a chosen model" to "all sufficiently supported preference-structure instances across a structured family of interpretable models".

Possible internal constructs:

- Structure Acceptability Index: probability or support level that a structure family is warranted by the evidence.
- Structure Necessity / Possibility: whether a ranking, sorting assignment, or selected action is stable across all or some supported structures.
- Central Structure Explanation: a representative structure configuration, not merely representative parameters.

Why this is more concrete:

- It becomes a method-internal output.
- It is analogous in shape to existing MCDA robustness work, but moves the uncertainty from parameters to structures.
- It directly answers the no-ground-truth decision problem: instead of pretending there is one true ranking, we report how much the recommendation depends on the selected preference structure.

Collision risks:

- SMAA and ROR already handle parameter uncertainty.
- Cinelli et al. already handle taxonomy-based MCDA method recommendation.
- Martyn & Kadziński already point to ensembles and activation of different MCDA approaches.

Survival condition:

We must show that none of these gives a data-supported, cross-structure acceptability analysis for decision recommendations.

### Candidate concrete object B: Structure-Discriminating Elicitation

Possible name:

- Adaptive Structure Elicitation, ASE.

Core idea:

Instead of only asking the DM for examples to estimate parameters, the system asks for the next assignment/comparison that maximally distinguishes among plausible preference structures.

Possible internal constructs:

- structure-discrimination score for candidate queries;
- expected reduction in structure ambiguity;
- query policy over alternatives or pairwise comparisons;
- stopping rule when the supported structure set is stable.

Why this is more concrete:

- It converts "adaptive" into an interactive MCDA procedure.
- It aligns with the constructive nature of MCDA.
- It can be compared against random query selection and fixed-model active learning.

Collision risks:

- Active learning for sorting and preference elicitation already exists.
- Cinelli et al. already use questions to reduce recommended methods, but taxonomy-based rather than evidence-driven.

Survival condition:

We must position the novelty as query selection for discriminating preference structures, not merely reducing parameter uncertainty inside a fixed model.

### Candidate concrete object C: Structure-Gated Preference Disaggregation

Possible name:

- Structure-Gated Preference Disaggregation, SG-PDA.

Core idea:

A single optimization or staged optimization learns a compact structure configuration:

- relevant criteria;
- monotonicity type;
- selected interactions;
- model family or aggregation form.

The gate activates higher-complexity structures only if they pass improvement, stability, and parsimony tests.

Why this is more concrete:

- It looks like a direct method paper.
- It can reuse existing learners as modules.
- It has a clear algorithm and ablations.

Weakness:

- It may still look like AutoML or model selection unless the output is decision-analytic rather than merely "best model".

## Provisional Judgment

If we follow peer-writing logic, the strongest direction is not:

> We propose a general adaptive MCDM framework.

It should become one of:

> We propose Preference-Structure Acceptability Analysis for data-supported robustness analysis across interpretable MCDA structures.

or:

> We propose Adaptive Structure Elicitation for selecting decision examples that distinguish among competing preference structures.

or:

> We propose Structure-Gated Preference Disaggregation, a learning procedure that jointly controls criteria relevance, monotonicity, and interaction activation.

The first option feels most decision-journal-native because it connects to ROR/SMAA and treats structural uncertainty as part of the recommendation. The second option is more interactive and potentially stronger but likely requires harder experiment design. The third option is easier to implement but has the highest risk of sounding like AutoML unless wrapped with decision-support outputs.

## Immediate Next Literature Checks

Before committing to any of the three, we need to verify:

1. Has anyone already proposed structure-level SMAA/ROR or model-family acceptability in MCDA?
2. Has active learning for multicriteria sorting selected queries to distinguish model families, not just parameters?
3. Has MCDA-MSS or related method-selection DSS used data/assignment examples rather than taxonomy descriptors?
4. Do recent deep MCDA preference learning papers operationalize cross-method ensembles as a decision-support output, or only mention it as future work?

Only after these checks should APSS be reframed again.
